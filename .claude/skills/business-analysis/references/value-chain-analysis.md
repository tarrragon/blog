# Value Chain Analysis: Upstream/Downstream Tracing and Structural Pressure Identification

> **Role**: Operational reference for the `business-analysis` skill. Consult when analyzing a company's position within its industry value chain, evaluating franchise systems, or investigating related-party transactions.

## Three-Sided Squeeze Identification

At every level of a value chain, participants face pressure from three directions. Identify each direction's specific form to understand the structural constraint on margins.

| Direction | Pressure Form | Identification Questions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Upstream | Supplier controls input costs, pricing, or specifications | Can the company freely choose or switch suppliers? Is input pricing market-determined or contract-locked? |
| Downstream | Customer controls end pricing through alternatives or price sensitivity | What is the customer's switching cost? How many alternatives exist within the customer's decision radius? |
| Lateral | Competitors or platforms take a cut or compress pricing | Are there platform commissions? Is there intense same-category competition? Can competitors easily replicate the offering? |

### Cross-Layer Recurrence

The same three-sided squeeze structure recurs at different levels of the same value chain. A franchise breakfast store is squeezed by its franchisor (upstream), consumers (downstream), and delivery platforms (lateral). Its ingredient supplier is squeezed by grain markets (upstream), supermarkets and restaurants (downstream), and peer processors (lateral) — with margins compressed to 15-21%.

Recognizing cross-layer recurrence reveals that margin pressure at one level is not a company-specific failure but a structural characteristic of the value chain position. The escape paths are also structurally similar across levels: integrate upstream, integrate downstream, or achieve scale.

## Franchise System Dual-Perspective Analysis

### Franchisee Perspective (Four-Layer Analysis)

Evaluate from the store operator's viewpoint:

1. **Report reading**: Identify denominator (store revenue vs customer spending), adjust for hidden costs (depreciation, insurance, renovation, hidden franchise fees in ingredient markup).
2. **Channel and cost decisions**: Calculate contribution margin per channel, distinguish fixed from variable costs, verify cost benchmarks match the operating model (owner-operated vs manager-operated).
3. **Investment assessment**: Separate capital return from labor return. If the owner stops working, does the capital alone earn an acceptable rate?
4. **Structural evaluation**: Map value chain position (three-sided squeeze), identify structural ceilings (pricing, cost, capacity), assess competitive moat (entry barriers).

### Franchisor Perspective (Four Revenue Lines)

Evaluate from the parent company's viewpoint:

| Revenue Line | Source | Typical Characteristics |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ingredient/material supply | Central factory or group procurement → franchise stores | Often the largest line (40-80% of revenue); recurring; margin depends on processing depth |
| Franchise fees | One-time payment from new franchisees | Non-recurring; grows only with net new store openings |
| Royalties/management fees | Ongoing percentage of franchisee revenue or fixed monthly fee | Recurring; scales with store count x per-store revenue |
| Direct-operated stores | Company-owned store revenue | Higher margin per store but capital-intensive |

A healthy franchisor derives most revenue from ingredient supply and royalties (recurring), not franchise fees (one-time). High franchise fee concentration signals the company relies on opening new stores rather than growing existing ones.

### Reverse Analysis: Franchisee Evaluating the Franchisor

Before signing a franchise agreement, use the franchisor's public financials to assess the deal:
- Is the franchisor publicly listed? If so, read the annual report.
- What is the franchisor's gross margin on ingredient supply? The gap between this margin and independent market pricing is the implicit franchise fee embedded in ingredient costs.
- What is the franchisor's store closure rate? Compare annual closures to annual openings — a high closure rate signals operational challenges that affect franchisee success.
- Is the brand in expansion, maturity, or decline phase? This determines the franchisee's growth opportunity and competitive pressure.

## Value-Chain Span Explains Margin (and Limits Comparability)

Gross-margin differences between companies in the same industry are often structural — a function of how much of the value chain each company captures internally — not a measure of operational efficiency. Before attributing a margin gap to one company being "better run," classify each by its value-chain span:

| Span | What the company does | Gross margin captures |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Full-process (vertical) | Buys raw inputs, does every processing step in-house, distributes | Every value-add layer's markup (raw → processed → distributed) |
| Resale of pre-processed goods | Buys already-processed inputs, often from a group affiliate, resells to stores | Only the layers after the input was processed — upstream markup already stripped out |
| External OEM / open procurement | Buys finished goods from independent suppliers | Only the brand/distribution margin; processing markup stays with the supplier |

Worked contrast: two same-industry franchise parents reported 36% and 27% gross margin. The 9-point gap is not "the first is more efficient." The 36% company runs a full-process central factory — every markup layer from raw input to finished product stays on its statement. The 27% company buys already-processed goods from a group affiliate — the processing markup was already taken upstream, on the affiliate's statement. The two margins measure different spans of the same value chain.

**Comparability guardrail**: gross margins across different value-chain spans are not directly comparable as efficiency indicators. A full-process producer and a reseller in the "same industry" are not margin peers. To compare efficiency, either (a) compare companies with the same span, or (b) for a group with upstream affiliates, consolidate the upstream and downstream margins before comparing to an integrated competitor (otherwise the group's true margin is understated — see Related Party Transaction Analysis). Reading a reseller's lower margin as inefficiency, or a full-process producer's higher margin as pricing power, is a category error when the spans differ.

This is the positive-framing complement to the transfer-pricing red flag: transfer pricing asks "is margin being shifted between affiliates?"; value-chain span asks "how much of the chain does this single entity's margin even cover?" The span answer reframes what the raw margin number means before any peer comparison begins.

## Related Party Transaction Analysis

### Identification

Related party transactions exist when companies under the same group trade with each other — parent supplying materials to subsidiary, sibling companies sharing services. These are disclosed in financial statement footnotes for listed companies.

### Profit Transfer Estimation Procedure

1. Identify the upstream group company and its gross margin.
2. Identify independent peers in the same business (same product, similar scale) and their gross margins.
3. Calculate the difference: if the group upstream company's margin is 5 percentage points above independent peers, that 5% may represent profit transferred from the downstream company's cost structure.
4. Estimate the absolute amount: apply the margin differential to the intercompany transaction volume.

**Investor implication**: Analyzing only the downstream company's financials understates the group's total profitability. The upstream company holds extracted margin that belongs economically to the integrated operation. Evaluate group-level returns, not just individual entity returns.

### Warning Signs

- Intercompany transactions exceeding 20% of revenue warrant detailed pricing investigation.
- If the downstream company's margin is significantly below independent peers while the upstream company's margin is above peers, transfer pricing is likely.
- Frequent restructuring of intercompany pricing terms coinciding with earnings targets is a red flag.

## Vertical Integration Comparison

Two dominant models for downstream integration:

| Dimension | Direct-Operated (e.g., CP Foods/卜蜂) | Franchise (e.g., Super Qin-Young Qin/超秦-揚秦) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Upstream | Self-owned farms, high feed self-sufficiency | Contract farms, AI-managed but not owned |
| Midstream | Self-owned slaughter + processing | Self-owned slaughter + custom cutting |
| Downstream | Company-owned retail brands + B2B institutional supply | Spun-off subsidiary with franchise model (1,000+ stores) |
| Capital model | Heavy-asset throughout (each store requires company capital) | Heavy upstream, light downstream (franchisees bear store capital) |
| Moat | B2B supply chain lock-in (convenience store OEM, tech company cafeterias) | Brand franchise network + customized supply capability |
| Expansion bottleneck | Capital expenditure limits store count growth | Quality control limits brand consistency at scale |

**Key trade-off**: Direct operation yields higher per-unit margin but slower expansion. Franchising expands faster but splits margin with franchisees. Both effectively consume upstream capacity — the choice depends on whether the company's constraint is capital (favor franchise) or quality control capability (favor direct).

## International Commodity Supply Chain

When the target company depends on imported raw materials, assess supply chain geography as a risk layer.

### Import Dependency Assessment

| Check | How to assess | Risk signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Self-sufficiency ratio | Domestic production / total consumption | < 20% = high import dependency |
| Source concentration | Top-2 import countries as % of total | > 70% = geographic concentration risk |
| Commodity type | Storable (grains) vs perishable (fresh meat) | Storable allows strategic inventory; perishable does not |
| Price transparency | Publicly traded commodity (futures exist) vs private negotiation | Futures-traded = price hedging possible; private = less transparency |

### Transmission Path Mapping

Map how an origin event reaches the target company's cost structure:

1. **Origin event** → production/export reduction at source country
2. **International price** → commodity futures or spot price increase
3. **Import cost** → price increase amplified by shipping cost and currency movement
4. **Domestic production cost** → feed/material cost increase in the target company
5. **End product price** → ability to pass through depends on market structure (competitive = hard to pass; monopoly = easy)

Three cost factors can hit simultaneously (as in 2020-2022 Taiwan animal feed: raw material +100%, shipping +500%, currency -18%). Assess each independently; compounding is the norm, not the exception.

### Procurement Strategy Matrix

| Strategy | Mechanism | Applicable when | Cost | Electronics industry equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Strategic inventory | Stockpile at low prices, consume during spikes | Storable commodity, warehouse available | Capital tie-up + storage | "Confirmed demand" tier in demand-tiered stocking |
| Long-term contract | Lock price with supplier for fixed period | Stable supply relationship, creditworthy parties | Forfeit downside benefit if prices drop | NCNR orders |
| Futures hedging | Use derivatives to lock future purchase price | Commodity has liquid futures market | Transaction cost + basis risk | Rarely used in electronics |
| Geographic diversification | Source from multiple countries | Quality specs allow substitution | Qualification cost + management complexity | Second-source strategy |
| Vertical integration | Own upstream operations (farms, processing) | Sufficient capital, technically feasible | Large CapEx, management complexity | Foundry owning its own fab |

Select strategy based on commodity characteristics: storable + futures-traded → strategic inventory + hedging; perishable + no futures → diversification + vertical integration.

## PR Claim Validation

When a company actively promotes transformation narratives (digital transformation, AI adoption, aggressive expansion), cross-validate every claim against financial data.

### Validation Checklist

| Claim Type | Validation Method |
| --- | --- |
| "Productivity improved by X%" | Determine if X% is from the best-performing pilot store or fleet-wide average. Pilot results are typically 2-3x the fleet average improvement. |
| "Plan to open N stores this year" | Compare to historical actual vs target: if target is 100-150 but actual net increase is 55-63, the gap (40-50 closures per year) is not prominently disclosed. |
| "Revenue grew Y%" | Decompose into new store contribution vs same-store growth. If most growth comes from new stores while same-store is flat, the growth story is weaker. |
| "Margin improved" | Decompose per the external-shock reference: how much is structural vs windfall vs one-time? |

### Additional Risk Signals in Transformation Narratives

- **Brand concentration**: Single brand contributing 80%+ of revenue is concentration risk, regardless of how well that brand is performing.
- **Second brand trajectory**: If the company promotes multi-brand strategy but the second brand's revenue is declining, the diversification narrative is not supported by data.
- **Store quality variance**: If product quality varies across stores (taste inconsistency, SOP execution gaps), the brand's value proposition erodes at scale — contradicting narratives about standardization and digital control.
